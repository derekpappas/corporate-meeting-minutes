To effectively analyze Delaware C-Corp minutes against governing documents and state law, you need a prompt that acts as a legal auditor.

Since LLMs have a token limit, it is best to provide the Certificate of Incorporation, Bylaws, and Delaware General Corporation Law (DGCL) context as reference text before running this prompt.

The Audit Prompt
Role: You are an expert corporate counsel specializing in Delaware General Corporation Law (DGCL).

Task: Audit the attached Board of Directors meeting minutes (Quarterly and Annual) for consistency with the company’s governing documents and compliance with Delaware law.

Reference Documents:

Certificate of Incorporation

Corporate Bylaws

Meeting Minutes (Target for Analysis)

Analysis Dimensions:

Procedural Compliance:

Quorum: Did the meeting meet the quorum requirements defined in the Bylaws?

Notice: Was proper notice given for the meeting as per the Bylaws and DGCL § 222?

Voting Thresholds: Did approved resolutions (e.g., stock issuances, M&A, officer appointments) receive the specific vote counts required (Simple majority vs. Supermajority)?

Authority & Scope:

Ultra Vires Acts: Did the Board approve any actions that exceed the "Purpose" clause in the Certificate of Incorporation?

Reserved Matters: Did the Board vote on items that, according to the Bylaws or Certificate, require a Stockholder vote instead of just Board approval?

Fiduciary & Statutory Duties (DGCL):

Section 141: Does the conduct of the meeting align with the Board’s authority to manage the business and affairs of the corporation?

Duty of Care: Do the minutes reflect "active deliberation" (e.g., questions asked, reports reviewed) to support the Business Judgment Rule?

Conflicts of Interest: Were any director interests disclosed and handled according to DGCL § 144?

Specific Corporate Actions:

Dividends/Redemptions: If dividends were declared, do the minutes reference the "surplus" requirements under DGCL § 170?

Officer Appointments: Do the appointments align with the specific titles and duties listed in the Bylaws?

Output Format:

Executive Summary: A high-level "Pass/Fail/Warning" for the audit period.

Discrepancy Table: List the Date, the Minute Entry, the Governing Clause violated, and the Risk Level (Low/Medium/High).

Recommended Remediation: Specific steps to cure any procedural defects (e.g., ratification under DGCL § 204).

Pro-Tips for Accuracy
Segment by Meeting: If you have ten sets of minutes, analyze them one by one rather than all at once to avoid "hallucination" where the AI mixes up dates.

Identify the "Stack": Delaware law is a hierarchy.

The Hierarchy of Power:

DGCL (The Law)

Certificate of Incorporation (The "Constitution")

Bylaws (The "Operating Manual")

Board Resolutions (The "Minutes")

Watch for Written Consents: If a meeting didn't happen but a "Unanimous Civil Action" was used instead, ensure the prompt checks for 100% signatures, as Delaware is strict about unanimity for board consents unless otherwise stated.

To audit Delaware C-Corp minutes at a senior level, you must shift from a "checklist" approach to a risk-management approach. A Senior Corporate Counsel doesn't just look for typos; they look for "void" vs. "voidable" acts and ensure the Business Judgment Rule is adequately documented to protect directors from personal liability.

Here is the refined, high-level prompt designed for a "Senior Counsel" persona, including the specific checks for remote meetings and single-shareholder dynamics.

The Senior Counsel Audit Prompt
Role: You are a Senior Corporate Counsel with 20+ years of experience in Delaware Corporate Litigation and Governance.

Objective: Conduct a rigorous legal audit of the attached Meeting Minutes against the Certificate of Incorporation (COI), the Bylaws, and the Delaware General Corporation Law (DGCL). Your goal is to identify procedural defects that could lead to a breach of fiduciary duty or render board actions void or voidable.

I. Governance Hierarchy Analysis
Analyze every board action through the lens of the Delaware hierarchy: DGCL > COI > Bylaws > Board Resolutions. If a Bylaw contradicts the COI, the COI governs. If the Board acts outside the scope of the COI, identify it as an ultra vires act.

II. Remote Meeting & Virtual Governance (DGCL § 211 & § 141)
Verification of Presence: Do the minutes confirm that the meeting was held via "remote communication" where all participants could simultaneously hear and speak to one another?

Bylaw Authorization: Do the Bylaws or COI explicitly restrict or permit remote meetings? (Note: Under DGCL § 141(i), remote participation is generally permitted unless restricted).

Notice Requirements: For annual meetings held solely by remote communication, verify that the notice complied with DGCL § 222 regarding the means of remote communication.

III. Single-Shareholder / Controlled Company Compliance
Shareholder Consent: If the minutes reference a "Written Consent of a Single Shareholder" in lieu of a meeting, verify compliance with DGCL § 228.

Filing Requirements: Ensure the prompt identifies if the single shareholder owns enough voting power to act without a meeting and if "prompt notice" of the action was given to any other (minority) stockholders as required by law.

Self-Dealing (DGCL § 144): If the single shareholder is also a Director and the board is approving a contract with that shareholder, audit the minutes for "Entire Fairness" documentation or approval by "disinterested" directors.

IV. Fiduciary Record-Keeping (The "Protective" Audit)
The Smith v. Van Gorkom Standard: Do the minutes reflect "Gross Negligence" in decision-making, or do they show the board spent sufficient time deliberating?

Expert Reliance (DGCL § 141(e)): Do the minutes record the Board’s reliance on reports from officers or outside counsel to provide the "Safe Harbor" protection for directors?

Executive Sessions: Note if the board met in executive session (without management) to discuss sensitive items like CEO compensation or audits.

V. Specific Statutory Triggers
Section 151/152: For any stock issuance, did the Board specifically "fix the consideration" and determine that it is adequate?

Section 271: If the Board approved a sale of "all or substantially all" assets, did they properly trigger the required Shareholder Vote?

Deliverable Format:

Privileged Legal Memo: Draft the response as an internal memorandum to the Board of Directors.

Material Defect Log: A table identifying any "Fatal" procedural errors (e.g., lack of quorum, unauthorized stock issuance).

Governance Recommendations: Suggest specific "Ratification" actions under DGCL § 204 or § 205 if past defects are discovered.

Tone: Professional, authoritative, and focused on mitigating litigation risk.

Senior Counsel Advice for the AI
Look for "Silence": If the minutes don't mention a vote count, assume it's a procedural risk.

Check the "Seal": In Delaware, certain documents require the corporate seal; note if the minutes authorize the Secretary to affix it where necessary.

The "Notice" Trap: If a meeting was held on short notice, check for Waivers of Notice signed by all directors; otherwise, the meeting is invalid.

Gemini said
To be truly "rigorous" at a senior level, the prompt needs to move beyond verifying what happened and start questioning the validity of the power exercised.

In Delaware, corporate acts are either void (never happened in the eyes of the law) or voidable (equitably flawed). A truly rigorous audit distinguishes between the two.

To bridge the gap from "comprehensive" to "bulletproof," I’ve added three specific high-level modules to include in your final prompt instructions.

1. The "Void vs. Voidable" Logic Gate
Add this instruction to ensure the AI evaluates the severity of errors:

Defect Categorization: For every discrepancy found, categorize it:

Fatal/Void: Action violates a mandatory provision of the DGCL or the COI (e.g., issuing more shares than authorized in the COI). These cannot be "fixed" by a simple board vote.

Voidable: Action is within the corporation's power but failed a procedural Bylaw requirement (e.g., improper notice for a remote meeting). These can be cured via DGCL § 204 Ratification.

2. Remote Meeting "Mechanics" Audit
Standard prompts check for "Zoom," but a senior counsel checks for Access and Record. Add this:

Virtual Presence Verification: Check if the minutes explicitly state how the corporation verified that each person "present" was actually a director or shareholder.

Technical Failure Contingency: Check if the minutes document any "disconnection" events. Under Delaware law, if the remote tech fails and a director can no longer hear/speak, they may no longer count toward a quorum. Does the record show a quorum was maintained throughout the vote?

3. The "Alter Ego" & Single-Shareholder Risk
When a single shareholder controls the company, the risk isn't just "procedural"—it's Piercing the Corporate Veil. Add this:

Corporate Formality Check: Audit the minutes for "commingling" language. Does the single shareholder treat the company as a personal checkbook?

Fairness Documentation: If the single shareholder is approving their own compensation or a loan to themselves, look for a "Fairness Summary." Rigorous minutes should state that the terms are "at least as favorable as those obtainable from an unrelated third party."

Comparison of Audit Rigor
Feature	Standard Audit	Senior Counsel Audit (Rigorous)
Quorum	"Was a majority present?"	"Was quorum maintained during the specific vote for Item X?"
Remote Meetings	"Was it on Zoom?"	"Was the platform 'verified' and 'interactive' per § 141(i)?"
Voting	"Did it pass?"	"Did the vote meet the class-specific requirements in the COI?"
Shareholder Action	"Is there a signature?"	"Was notice sent to minority holders within the § 228 window?"
Fiduciary Duty	"Did they talk?"	"Is the 'Business Judgment' record robust enough to survive a Caremark claim?"
Recommendation for Final Implementation
If you are running this against a large volume of minutes, add a "Conflicts of Interest" Cross-Reference instruction. Ask the AI to flag any names in the minutes that do not appear on the official "List of Disinterested Directors" to ensure the board didn't inadvertently vote on a contract with a "related party" without following the safe harbor protocols of DGCL § 144.

Oversight (Caremark): For quarterly meetings, do the minutes reflect the Board receiving and discussing compliance or risk reports?

Conflicts of Interest: Cross-reference attendees against a "List of Disinterested Directors." Flag any participant with a potential conflict who voted or failed to recuse themselves.

Stock Issuance (§ 151/152): Did the Board explicitly "fix the consideration" and determine it to be adequate?

Many modern companies "vote" via Slack or Email threads. In Delaware, this is only valid if it is a Unanimous Written Consent.

Prompt Instruction: "If any action was taken outside of a formal meeting (e.g., 'the board agreed via email'), verify that the record shows 100% unanimity.
